As measured

◈ Live readout · 2026-07-15

Unlike the T480s, I wrote this page from the machine itself, so every number above is what the hardware actually reports — not a listing.

The battery is the story. It ships as Lenovo’s Power Bridge pair: a 23 Wh internal plus a 23 Wh hot-swappable external. BAT0 reports 13.79 Wh against a 23.2 Wh design — 59.4% health. BAT1 reports present: 1 but returns no capacity at all and energy_now: 0: the external pack is in the bay and dead. So of the 46.4 Wh this laptop shipped with, about 13.8 Wh survives, and it is permanently on AC.

Detected: 20BWS0HT00 · BIOS JBET51WW 1.16 · SANYO 45N1773 internal pack · PNY CS900 500 GB over SATA. The one thing I could not read is the RAM layout — dmidecode needs root, so whether the 8 GB is 4 soldered + 4 in the SODIMM slot is still unconfirmed.

The noise is the fan, not the drive. There’s an intermittent vibration under load, and the two suspects were the disk and the fan. It can’t be the disk: /sys/block/sda/queue/rotational reports 0 and the drive is a PNY CS900 SSD. No platters, no spindle, no moving parts — an SSD physically cannot make a mechanical noise. That leaves the fan, by elimination.

/proc/acpi/ibm/fan backs it up. At idle it sits at 0 rpm (40–49 °C) — genuinely stopped, not just quiet, which is why the noise is intermittent rather than constant. Under four-thread load the package climbs to 81 °C and the fan ramps 0 → 2,388 → 4,032 rpm, settling around 3,900. It spins up, it moves air, it holds temperature. So it isn’t dead — it’s an eleven-year-old sleeve bearing that rattles once it’s turning. Annoying, not fatal, and the good news is that the failure mode is a $15 part rather than the storage.

Why it lives on the fridge

Every other machine on this list earns its place by being useful. This one earns its place by sitting on top of the refrigerator and ringing a doorbell that does not exist.

I paid nothing for it — a friend just gave it to me. That’s worth putting next to what it cost when it was new: in 2015 the T450s started at $881, and that base price bought a Core i5-5200U with 4 GB of RAM and a 500 GB spinning disk. This one has the i7 — a tier above even the i5-5300U that cost $100 extra — so new, it was comfortably a four-figure laptop. Ten years later it is a doorbell.

The battery made the decision. A 2015 ThinkPad whose internal pack is down to 59% health and whose hot-swappable external pack is completely dead is not a laptop any more — it has about 13.8 Wh of the 46.4 Wh it left the factory with. It cannot leave a wall socket. But a machine that can’t be portable is still a perfectly good computer, and an i7 with 8 GB of RAM and a 500 GB SSD is an absurd amount of hardware to leave in a drawer.

So it went on top of the fridge, lid shut, plugged in.

It is not a good laptop, and I want to be honest about that. It’s one of the most underpowered machines here — only the ARM Chromebooks sit below it — and the screen is the worst of the lot: the base TN panel, which Notebookcheck measured at 400:1 contrast against the IPS option’s 700:1, with “color inversions and other image errors” the moment you move vertically. That’s the joke of the spec sheet: the $70 FHD upgrade was the IPS upgrade. Whoever ordered this took the fastest CPU on the menu and the cheapest screen — and a decade later the CPU is irrelevant and the screen is the thing you notice.

None of which matters for what it does now. There’s no USB-C on a 2015 ThinkPad, and it doesn’t need any: it’s docked above the fridge with speakers hanging off it, and the ports it does have — three USB-A and a headphone jack — are exactly the ports that job wants. And unlike the T480s, I’d never dual-boot Windows onto this one. The T480s had the headroom to make that worth the restart-and-update tax. This one doesn’t, and it has precisely one job.

But it works. That’s the whole point of the page.

HandleLidSwitch=ignore · HandleLidSwitchDocked=ignore

Two lines in /etc/systemd/logind.conf are the only reason this works. Without them, closing the lid suspends the machine and the whole thing is just a laptop on a fridge.

The payload is one file — ~/Sounds/doorbell.m4a — and one command:

ssh tp-450s.local 'ffplay -nodisp -autoexit ~/Sounds/doorbell.m4a'

That’s the entire application. No service, no daemon, no framework — sshd and ffplay, both of which were already there. A guest hears the doorbell, gets up, walks to the front door, and finds nobody there. The 14″ TN panel it shipped with has not displayed anything in months, and the 3.3 lb chassis weight is a purely academic figure.

The ssh half is the part that makes it a prank rather than a party trick. Without it the doorbell can only be rung from the machine itself — which, for a computer deliberately hidden on top of a fridge, rather defeats the point.
