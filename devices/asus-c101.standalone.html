This one’s aarch64 (ARM), so it’s a different world from the x86 boxes: the CPU is a hexa-core Rockchip OP1 — a binned RK3399 with 2× Cortex-A72 big cores and 4× Cortex-A53 little ones — paired with a Mali-T860 MP4 GPU. I’ll drop in the real detected values next time I’m on it: the eMMC model, battery health and cycle count, and whatever a Crostini (or Linux) shell actually reports for cores and RAM.

Why I got it

I’d always wanted this one. The little 10-inch ASUS Flip has been on my list for years — it’s basically the pocket-sized sibling of my C302 — and in June 2026 I finally grabbed one. What I couldn’t believe was that it was brand new. A sealed, never-opened C101PA in 2026, for $109.86 all-in? That’s amazing. For a machine I’d wanted this long, paying about a hundred bucks for a new one felt almost unfair — it listed at $299 when it launched in 2017.

Brand new, in 2026, for $110. A device I’d wanted for years — and it showed up sealed in the box. I still can’t get over it.

The battery life is the other half of the love. These little ARM Chromebooks just go — all-day, don’t-think-about-the-charger endurance, the same thing that sold me on the C302. Silent, fanless, tiny, and it lasts forever.

The catch is the ARM chip. I’m trying to see if I can get real Linux on here, but aarch64 makes it a narrower path than the x86 machines — a lot of the usual tooling and distros just don’t line up the way they do on Intel. I might just stick with ChromeOS on this one and let it be the featherweight it already is.

My only real gripe is the same one that’s on every laptop now: both USB-C ports sit on the same (right) side. My C302 is the only machine I own that does it right — one port on the left, one on the right. Everything since has clustered them on one edge. Small thing, but I notice it every time I plug in from the wrong side.
